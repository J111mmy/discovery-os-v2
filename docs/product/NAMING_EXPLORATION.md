# DiscOS Naming Exploration

Working naming strategy for the DiscOS Vercel app. This is a brand strategy artifact only.

## Current Name Assessment

Current name:

**DiscOS**

Likely meaning:

Discovery OS.

What works:

- Compact and memorable.
- Carries internal continuity from Discovery OS.
- Implies a system, not a single point tool.
- The `OS` suffix fits the product's ambition as an intelligence layer across discovery work.

What does not work:

- Pronunciation is ambiguous: "disco", "disc O S", or "discovery OS."
- "Disco" associations are playful and may undercut the serious evidence/confidence value.
- `OS` can sound too infrastructure-heavy for PMs and GTM buyers.
- Search and brand confusion risk is real because DISCO is an AI-powered legal/eDiscovery software company.
- It does not communicate evidence, traceability, confidence, source lineage, or defensible outputs.

Recommendation:

Keep `DiscOS` as an internal or temporary product name while testing external names. Do not commit it as the external brand until trademark, domain, SEO, pronunciation, and buyer-comprehension checks are complete.

## Naming Strategy

The name should point toward the value DiscOS actually creates:

- Evidence becomes usable.
- Claims stay connected to sources.
- Teams move faster because outputs are defensible.
- Customer discovery becomes organizational memory.
- Product and GTM work from the same evidence base.

The name should avoid sounding like:

- A generic AI copilot
- A meeting recorder
- A research folder
- A legal/eDiscovery tool
- A roadmap/prioritization board
- A lightweight feedback widget

## Naming Territories

### 1. Proof And Defensibility

This territory emphasizes confidence, support, and decisions that can withstand scrutiny.

Strongest fit when the promise is "outputs people can defend."

Candidates:

- Proofline
- Prooftrail
- Proofbase
- Proofmark

- Claimline

**Proofline**

Why: It suggests a visible line from evidence to output. It is simple, ownable-feeling, and commercially serious without sounding legalistic.

Risk: It may be read as compliance/audit software if the surrounding copy is too formal.

## Friendly And Creative Expansion

The first-pass names are useful because they point directly at proof, evidence, and traceability. The downside is that many of them feel dry. They describe the system more than they invite people into it.

A better brand may need to feel more like:

- A shared place where teams make sense of customer reality
- A path through messy evidence
- A memory that helps the organization learn
- A companion for discovery work
- A way to turn fragments into something people can act on

These names still need legal, domain, and market checks. They are creative directions, not availability claims.

### More Serious Names To Keep

These are still useful, but I would now treat them as the conservative set rather than the preferred set.

### 1. Proofline

Positioning fit: Very strong.

Best line:

Proofline turns customer evidence into defensible product and GTM outputs.

Why it could win:

- Clear link to proof.
- Implies traceability without sounding technical.
- Works across product, research, and GTM.
- Strong for a demo where claims are linked to source evidence.

Watchouts:

- Needs trademark/domain screening.
- Must avoid drifting into compliance language.

### 2. Tracebase

Positioning fit: Strong.

Best line:

Tracebase is the traceable intelligence layer for product discovery.

Why it could win:

- Closest to the system architecture.
- Strong product/technical credibility.
- Makes the evidence graph feel durable.

Watchouts:

- May feel less emotionally appealing to GTM buyers.
- Could sound like a database or observability tool.

### 3. Groundline

Positioning fit: Strong.

Best line:

Groundline keeps every product decision connected to real customer evidence.

Why it could win:

- Softer and more strategic than proof/trace language.
- Good if the brand should feel mature, calm, and broad.
- Works against generic AI by implying grounded outputs.

Watchouts:

- Needs explanation.
- Less immediately specific than Proofline.

### 4. Citable

Positioning fit: Strong, if available.

Best line:

Citable helps teams create product and GTM artifacts that cite the evidence behind every claim.

Why it could win:

- Instant comprehension.
- Very close to the artifact value.
- Memorable.

Watchouts:

- Likely availability challenges.
- Can skew academic/legal.

### 5. Claimwise

Positioning fit: Medium-strong.

Best line:

Claimwise verifies what your customer evidence actually supports.

Why it could win:

- Strong for claim verification and defensible outputs.
- Distinct from repository language.

Watchouts:

- Narrower than the full product.
- Could sound like a consultancy or fact-checking tool.

## Names To Avoid

- Disco / DiscOS as final external brand without legal/search validation.
- Anything too close to DISCO, Dovetail, Productboard, Jira, Enterpret, Sourcegraph, Ground Truth, or Glean.
- Names centered on "copilot," "AI," "insight," or "research repo."
- Names that make the product sound like only transcription, storage, or prioritization.

## Next Validation Steps

1. Run trademark and domain checks for the shortlist.
2. Test pronunciation and recall with five target buyers.
3. Show each name with the same one-line description and ask what product they expect.
4. Score each against: clarity, ownability, seriousness, GTM range, product fit, and risk of legal/research confusion.
5. Keep the category descriptor independent from the chosen name so the strategy can survive a rename.
